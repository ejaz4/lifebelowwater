Home page

home.html

The homepage is a page which is presented to the user after the splash screen. It is designed to be visually appealing and quickly explain the purpose of SeaGate as well as provide a call to action for the user to get involved.

The current call to action is to volunteer labelled as "Help now", as we would rather point the user to volunteer rather than donate or give money.

The homepage starts off showcasing a video of a man who is pulling a bucket out of the water, which is relevant to the goal of the charity.

The video is contained in a <section> which has a primary purpose of dividing the rest of the content away from this page, and to help layer text on-top of the video to use the space extremely efficiently.

In order to layer the text on-top of the video, I opted to use the following CSS rule: position: relative on the section, and position: absolute on the video.

How this works is that position: relative makes sure that children inside of it can only be positioned inside of it and not across the whole DOM, which makes it easier for me to position the text on-top of the video as the video stayed attached to the section at all times.

This also means I don't need to disrupt the normal flow of the DOM by positioning elements that don't exist due to position: absolute or position: fixed.

Just below it, the page content begins.

I wanted something eye-catching and something simple, so I addded a beautiful rainbow that I built in a design tool called Figma and applied it as a background-image that stays at the top centre of the section, always.

Some simple words explaining who we are and what we do, as well as three cards that explain our business model and our relation to the United Nations in steps.

That ensures that visitors are aware of who we are, what we do, why we do it, and who we do it for - cruicial information to keep the user engaged.

Inside each of the 3 cards, there is an image with an icon: an institution, a spanner and a heart - which shows how changes come from the UN and end up with world love being the end result of all of our actions.

There is a beautiful shadow underneath all of the card to distinguish them and on mobile and lower resolution screens only one card is visible at a time, and you can scroll left and right where the scroll snaps to the start of the cards to avoid content not being visible at all.

Below that I have the links to the content page in a creative way.

I instructed my team members to create their content pages like news or blog articles to which I created a section named Reads From SeaGate which contains a grid of posts.

The posts link to the content pages of each student in their respective order and the post previews contain the image and titles of the content pages.

Throughout the whole home page, I made use of flexbox, a very cool CSS technology which allows me to make children of a particular element move around exactly how I want them to, all while staying responsive.

Table page (Donate)

table.html

This page is designed to encourage users to donate.

It has been developed and designed to give users freedom and choice by showing and presenting flexible and free options for donating to the charity.

Minimalism and simplistic design was at heart during the development of the Table page.

In the table page, I have <main> set to be a flexbox, with all its children in the centre of the page, because I wanted a page with a table to be central on the screen, which meant the text should hover around the centre.

Inside <main>, I have <section> with a class I called .readable. The purpose of .readable is it is intended to optimise content inside of it to be as readable as possible by the user.

Some optimisations include, small amount of breathability and the most important of all: a width of 700 pixels, or approximately 30-50 characters per line.

This means that readers won't need to move their head left or right while reading. On mobile, it will just extend to the full width of the page.

I included a drawing that is an SVG of a ship sailing as a decorative illustrative that was provided from undraw.

Below, I wrote an explanation as to what the table is describing, and what the purpose of the table is.

The purpose is to ease the user into the donation table without surprising them with a load of data.

Hence, short paragraphs are used as they're lighter and easier to read.

As for the actual table, I made use of <table> for the table, <tr> for the table rows, <td> for the table data, and <th> for the table headers.

The table has all of its borders collapsed using border-collapse: collapsed so that all the borders combine and that the cells aren't avoiding each other.

In order to curve the table, I had to make use of a CSS selector called :first-child and :last-child to navigate myself to the top left and top right cells to apply border-radius to them.

The table itself is a two-way table, with features and benefits on the Y axis and the donation amount on the X axis.

The aim of the table is to show a reader what their donation would equate to and what they would receive in a donation if they were to donate a particular amount.

Inside, if a price point supported a benefit, I used an SVG of a checkmark, provided by lucide icons. If there was a catch to the feature, a piece of text would show next to it.

The table transitions upwards if you hover and the shadow concentrates on hover to show you a clearer border of where it starts and ends.

To promote transparency, I listed all the available payment methods, including a method using cash.

I felt it was appropriate to implement this using an unordered list as it is a list of items that has no particular order.

Content page

content_ST2.html

This page was designed to mimick a news article of the charity unveiling products at an event.

I used the same .readable CSS optimisation I used earlier in the donation page, but this time I wrapped the .readable section with a modified <main> which was mutated and adjusted to be a flexbox that was in the direction of a row using flex-direction: row; instead of a column like traditional HTML.

The article is off to the left and the navigation is on the right side showing off the different areas that the user can jump to.

The navigation is a <nav> that is position: sticky meaning it follows the browser so that the user can navigate at any point during their read to skip lesser interesting parts or get to parts that excite them the most.

Seperated by a gap, there is a Get To Top button in the navigation that I believe is in the right place. It is a simple click in a place the user can find without taking up even more space for the user.

On mobile, the navigation does not appear as both major mobile operating systems have methods of extracting text off the screen, and both major operating systems have the ability to jump to the top.

The headings are in order from h1 to h6 and the images have been labelled using alt text that are extremely descriptive.

The site adapts and responds very well to mobile and all major web browsers can successfully identify and activate their reader modes on the content page.

Header

The header is a vital part of the website.

It is shown on-top of everything and stays there forever so that the user can quickly navigate to different areas of the website.

When you visit a page that is accessible through the navigation, that page is highlighted in the navigation with a white highlight to indicate you're on that page to the user.

When you hover, a dark highlight shows to indicate you're able to click it.

When you scroll down on any page with the header, the header will collapse and become transparent to show more content. The colours of the header drown out to not distract the user as much and the logo becomes smaller.

On mobile, the navigation on the header is no longer shown and will show a hamburger menu instead, which when toggled, will show the same contents as the desktop navigation but in a stacked layout.

Click the logo at any time to return to the homepage.

Footer

The footer is displayed at the bottom of every page.

It displays vital links to content, validation and page editors from other students.

It is a two sided design with a nice logo and a copyright year on the left side, and on the right side all the links in beautiful font.
